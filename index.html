Background

A geologist who followed
microbes into the sea

My scientific path began with the solid Earth — rocks, minerals, deep time — studying Earth Science at Imperial College London. The longer I looked at the planet, the more I was drawn to its most dynamic surfaces: where geology, chemistry, biology, and climate converge.

An MSc in Climate Change at the University of Copenhagen shifted my focus toward the present and urgent. Before my PhD I worked as a research assistant on nanocatalytic microbial methanation in abandoned North Sea oil infrastructure — an early encounter with how microbes transform the materials we leave behind.

My PhD at Copenhagen's POSH Lab under Prof. Nicole Posth brought me to the coastal ocean and plastic pollution. I studied how plastic debris in the water column and sediment becomes colonised by microbial communities — the plastisphere — and how those communities interact with metals and minerals across redox environments. This included fieldwork along the Tanzanian coastline, extending plastisphere science into the understudied Western Indian Ocean.

I work across disciplines by necessity: field sampling, geochemical analysis, SEM-EDS, microbial sequencing, bioinformatics. The ocean's problems don't fit clean boxes, and neither do I.

Outside science, I paint — acrylics on black paper. Mostly big cats.

Research Focus

Plastisphere science at the
frontier of marine pollution

🌊

Plastic in the Water Column & Sediment

Field-based sampling and characterisation of plastic debris across marine coastal environments — tracking distribution, polymer type, and vertical transport from surface water through sediment across contrasting redox conditions.

Marine PollutionField SamplingSedimentology
🦠

The Plastisphere — Microbial Life on Plastic

Characterising microbial communities colonising plastic debris across a 12-month period. 16S rRNA sequencing reveals community composition, diversity, and enzymatic capacity — including genes indicative of plastic degradation and element cycling.

Microbial Ecology16S SequencingQIIME2
⚗️

Metal & Mineral Associations

Identifying biologically-controlled, biologically-induced, and abiotic minerals on plastic surfaces — silicon dioxide, iron and manganese oxides — and characterising how microbial biofilms drive their formation across redox environments.

BiogeochemistryTrace MetalsSEM-EDS
🔬

Microbial Metal Cycling Potential

Exploring the genetic capacity of plastisphere communities to cycle metals — functional genes involved in metal reduction, oxidation, and resistance — connecting genomic capacity to geochemical outcomes at plastic surfaces in the marine environment.

Functional GenomicsMetal CyclingBioinformatics

Why this matters for industry

Understanding what microbes do on plastic — and what plastic does to marine geochemistry — is foundational to designing better materials, improving plastic recovery, and assessing ecological risk. This work is directly relevant to circular economy innovation, environmental impact assessment, and marine conservation policy.

Outside the Lab

White acrylic on black —
painting the animals I want to protect

I paint in acrylics on black paper — almost always big cats. There is something about working in white on black that feels honest: you are pulling light out of darkness, which is not so different from fieldwork. I am drawn to apex predators the same way I am drawn to the ocean — they hold ecosystems together, and we are losing them. The paintings are personal, but they are not separate from the science.
